# Full Argument

Even with their impressive capabilities, current reasoning models still fall short of the depth and adaptability of human intelligence. Despite their apparent capacity for reasoning, these systems largely operate through **statistical prediction** rather than genuine **logical inference**.

What often appears to be logic is merely an *imitation* of it ‚Äî a probabilistic alignment of linguistic patterns that occasionally produces correct results by chance. This is why such models can solve some mathematical problems but fail unpredictably on others.

The analogy of a **broken clock** is apt: it tells the correct time twice a day not because it understands time, but because its fixed state happens to align with reality on rare occasions. Similarly, the frozen weights of current models constrain their reasoning capacity to what they have already seen, rather than allowing for true understanding.

---

## Beyond Probabilistic Architectures

To move beyond these limitations, progress likely depends on transcending purely probabilistic architectures. Building systems trained specifically for **structured logical reasoning** ‚Äî systems that can integrate input from both large language models and **dynamic interaction** with their environments ‚Äî may represent the next major step.

**Agentic models** are a move in this direction, adding layers of goal-directed behavior and memory to LLMs. However, they still rest on the same underlying architecture, which primarily enhances functionality without fundamentally advancing toward meaningful general intelligence.

---

## A Modular Alternative

An alternative path might lie in developing **modular architectures** composed of many specialized ‚Äúmicro-models,‚Äù each optimized for a narrow function and trained rapidly in real time.

In such systems, **catastrophic forgetting** could be reframed as a *feature* rather than a flaw: each micro-model would retain only the information relevant to its immediate purpose.

A higher-level **‚Äúscaffolding‚Äù model** would then coordinate these micro-models, integrating their outputs into coherent behavior.

This **distributed and adaptive structure** more closely mirrors the way biological organisms process information ‚Äî through specialized subsystems working in concert. If the ultimate goal is to approximate **human-like intelligence**, our artificial systems may need to evolve in a similar direction.

## Perspective on Progress

While technological advancements appear to be making exponential strides in the discovery and replication of the underlying functions that give us our intelligence, **life has had billions of years** to develop that intelligence.

So a single model ‚Äî even as large as a **1 Trillion Parameter Kimi-K2 model**, representing only about **1% of the synapses of a human brain** ‚Äî is still a long way from **artificial general intelligence**, and an infinitesimal distance from **artificial superintelligence**.
